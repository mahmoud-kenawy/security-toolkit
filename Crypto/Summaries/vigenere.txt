The Vigenère Cipher is a polyalphabetic substitution cipher that uses a keyword to apply a different Caesar shift to each letter of the plaintext, making it significantly harder to crack than simple Caesar.

How It Works:
The keyword is repeated (or trimmed) to match the length of the plaintext. Each letter of the plaintext is shifted by the corresponding keyword letter's position.

Encryption: C[i] = (P[i] + K[i]) mod 26
Decryption: P[i] = (C[i] - K[i] + 26) mod 26

Example (key = "KEY"):
  Plaintext:  H  E  L  L  O
  Key:        K  E  Y  K  E   (repeated)
  Shifts:    +10 +4 +24 +10 +4
  Ciphertext: R  I  J  V  S

Key Parameters:
- Keyword: any string of letters. Longer keywords = more security.
- Case-insensitive. Non-alpha characters pass through unchanged.

Strengths:
- Multiple Caesar shifts defeat simple frequency analysis.
- Keyspace grows with keyword length — much stronger than Caesar.
- Called "le chiffre indéchiffrable" (the indecipherable cipher) for centuries.

Weaknesses:
- Vulnerable to Kasiski examination (finding repeated keyword patterns).
- Broken by Charles Babbage and Friedrich Kasiski in the 19th century.
- Weak against modern statistical attacks with known keyword length.

Real-World Use:
- Precursor to the one-time pad (unbreakable if key is truly random and as long as message).
- Taught extensively in cryptography courses as a stepping stone to stream ciphers.
- Inspired modern symmetric key concepts.
