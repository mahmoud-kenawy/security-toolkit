The Caesar Cipher is one of the oldest and simplest substitution ciphers, named after Julius Caesar who used it for military communication. Each letter in the plaintext is shifted a fixed number of positions along the alphabet.

How It Works:
Encryption: C = (P + key) mod 26
Decryption: P = (C - key) mod 26
Where P is the plaintext letter position (A=0, Z=25) and key is the shift value.

Example (key = 4):
  Plaintext:  A T T A C K A T O N C E
  Ciphertext: E X X E G O E X S R G I

Key Parameters:
- Key (shift): an integer from 1 to 25.
- Key 13 is the special case "ROT13" — its own inverse.

Strengths:
- Simple to understand and implement.
- Works on any alphabet.

Weaknesses:
- Only 25 possible keys — trivially brute-forced.
- Vulnerable to frequency analysis (letter distributions are preserved).
- No resistance to modern cryptanalysis.

Real-World Use:
- ROT13 is still used in forums to hide spoilers.
- Historical context: Julius Caesar's communications.
- Educational introduction to symmetric cipher concepts.
