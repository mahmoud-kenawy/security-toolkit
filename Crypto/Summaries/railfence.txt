The Rail Fence Cipher is a transposition cipher that rearranges the plaintext by writing it in a zigzag pattern across a number of "rails" (rows), then reading off each rail from left to right.

How It Works:
Letters are placed diagonally on alternating rails, forming a zigzag. The cipher text is formed by reading each rail independently from top to bottom.

Example (key = 3 rails, plaintext = WEAREDISCOVERED):
  Rail 1: W . . . E . . . I . . . V . .
  Rail 2: . E . R . D . S . O . E . E .
  Rail 3: . . A . . . C . . . R . . . D

  Ciphertext: WEIVERDSOEEACRD (read rail by rail)

Decryption works by rebuilding the zigzag grid with placeholder positions, then filling in the ciphertext and reading off diagonally.

Key Parameters:
- Number of rails (key): an integer ≥ 2.
- More rails = more scrambling, but also more structure to exploit.

Strengths:
- Simple to implement.
- Changes character positions — not identities — so frequency analysis of letters is unchanged but patterns shift.

Weaknesses:
- Very small effective keyspace (only as many keys as rails, usually 2–10).
- Easily brute-forced or solved with pencil-and-paper.
- No substitution — every plaintext character appears unchanged in ciphertext.

Real-World Use:
- Historical telegraph ciphers.
- Used as a teaching example of transposition ciphers.
- Occasionally combined with substitution for layered classical ciphers.
