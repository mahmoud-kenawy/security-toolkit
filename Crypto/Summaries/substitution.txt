The Simple Substitution Cipher replaces each letter of the plaintext with a fixed corresponding letter from a scrambled key alphabet. Unlike Caesar (which shifts uniformly), each letter has its own unique mapping.

How It Works:
A 26-character key alphabet is defined. Every letter in the plaintext is replaced by the letter at the corresponding position in the key.

Example (key = "POIUYTRWEQASDFGHJKLMNBVCXZ"):
  Plaintext: A B C D E F ...
  Ciphertext: P O I U Y T ...

  Encrypting "HELLO":
  H→W, E→T (wait — let's trace: A→P, B→O, C→I, D→U, E→Y, F→T, G→R, H→W)
  HELLO → WTSSG

Decryption: find the ciphertext letter in the key alphabet and return the corresponding standard letter.

Key Parameters:
- Key: any permutation of the 26-letter alphabet (26! ≈ 4 × 10²⁶ possible keys).
- Non-alpha characters pass through unchanged.

Strengths:
- Enormous keyspace (26!) — brute force is completely infeasible.
- Easy to implement by hand.

Weaknesses:
- Single-letter frequency analysis breaks it in seconds for long texts.
- English letter frequencies (E, T, A, O...) are preserved.
- Digraph and trigraph analysis further accelerates breaking.

Real-World Use:
- Used in cryptoquote puzzles and newspaper cipher games.
- The basis of the Enigma machine's substitution step (with rotating rotors).
- Historical use in secret societies and early espionage.
